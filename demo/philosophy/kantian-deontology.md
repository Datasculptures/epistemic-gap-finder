Kantian deontology holds that moral action is governed by rational duty — the
categorical imperative — which requires acting only on maxims one could will
to become universal laws, and treating rational beings always as ends in
themselves and never merely as means.

It operates in ethics and moral metaphysics, arguing through pure practical
reason: the moral law is derived from rationality alone, independent of
consequences, desires, or empirical circumstances.

It produces a system of strict duties and perfect obligations, a framework for
evaluating the moral permissibility of actions regardless of outcome, and a
foundation for theories of rights and human dignity.

Kantian deontology does not evaluate actions by their consequences, does not
give weight to virtue or character as the primary moral category, and does not
engage with the social or relational dimensions of moral life beyond the
requirements of universal rational law.
