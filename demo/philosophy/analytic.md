Analytic philosophy is a tradition defined by its commitment to clarity,
logical rigour, and the use of formal methods — logic, language analysis, and
conceptual decomposition — to address philosophical problems, with a strong
emphasis on the philosophy of language, logic, mind, and science.

It operates through argument analysis, formal logical reconstruction, and
conceptual clarification, treating philosophy as continuous with science and
mathematics in its methods if not always its subject matter.

It produces precise formulations of philosophical problems, formal theories of
meaning, reference, truth, and knowledge, and a culture of philosophical
practice centred on the published argument and the seminar objection.

Analytic philosophy does not privilege the first-person perspective or lived
experience as primary data, is generally sceptical of grand systematic
metaphysics in the continental style, and resists philosophical writing that
prioritises literary or rhetorical effect over argumentative transparency.
