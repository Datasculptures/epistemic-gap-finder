Existentialism holds that existence precedes essence — that human beings are
radically free, defined not by a prior nature or divine purpose but by the
choices they make in a world that offers no inherent meaning.

It operates in ethics, ontology, and phenomenology, arguing through first-person
analysis of lived experience, anxiety, authenticity, and the confrontation with
absurdity, death, and freedom.

It produces a philosophy of radical individual responsibility, frameworks for
understanding bad faith and authenticity, and a literature of existential
crisis and resolution that shaped twentieth-century thought and art.

Existentialism does not ground ethics in universal rational law or aggregate
welfare, does not offer a systematic metaphysics of substance or causation, and
does not provide a political programme — though individual existentialists
developed varied and often conflicting political commitments.
