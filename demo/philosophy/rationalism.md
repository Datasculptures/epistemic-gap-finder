Rationalism holds that reason, independent of sensory experience, is the
primary source of genuine knowledge — that certain truths are known a priori,
that the mind contains innate ideas or structures, and that the deepest
features of reality are accessible to pure rational inquiry.

It operates in epistemology and metaphysics, arguing through deductive
demonstration: knowledge of God, substance, causation, and the self is
secured by rational intuition and proof rather than by observation.

It produces a systematic metaphysics, a priori foundations for mathematics and
logic, and a model of knowledge as the unfolding of necessary truths through
reason alone.

Rationalism does not treat sensory observation as the foundation of knowledge,
does not confine inquiry to what can be empirically tested, and in its
classical forms makes strong metaphysical claims about substance, God, and
the soul that empiricist traditions regard as overreach.
