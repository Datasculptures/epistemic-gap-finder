Empiricism holds that all knowledge of the world derives from sensory
experience — that the mind begins as a blank slate and that concepts, beliefs,
and scientific laws are built up from observation, perception, and induction
rather than innate ideas or pure reason.

It operates in epistemology and philosophy of science, arguing through analysis
of how knowledge is actually acquired: tracing concepts to their experiential
origins and subjecting claims to empirical test.

It produces a theory of knowledge grounded in observation, a sceptical critique
of claims that outrun experience, and the philosophical foundations of the
scientific method and evidence-based inquiry.

Empiricism does not ground knowledge in innate rational structures or a priori
principles, and in its strict forms it struggles to account for mathematical
necessity, causal necessity, and the uniformity of nature without appeal to
factors beyond experience.
