Stoicism holds that virtue — practical wisdom, justice, courage, and
temperance — is the only genuine good, and that external circumstances
including wealth, health, and reputation are indifferent to human flourishing.

It operates in ethics and psychology, arguing through reason, the discipline
of attention, and the practice of distinguishing what is within one's control
from what is not.

It produces a practice of equanimity, a framework for action under uncertainty,
and a training programme for the will that has been applied from ancient Rome
to contemporary cognitive behavioural therapy.

Stoicism does not deny that suffering occurs or that preferred outcomes exist —
it denies that suffering caused by external events is rationally necessary, and
it does not engage substantially with metaphysical questions about the nature
of being or the structure of knowledge.
