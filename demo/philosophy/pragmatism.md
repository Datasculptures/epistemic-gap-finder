Pragmatism holds that the meaning of any concept lies in its practical
consequences — that ideas are tools for action, and that truth, knowledge,
and value are to be evaluated by how well they help us navigate experience
and achieve our purposes.

It operates in epistemology, ethics, and philosophy of language, arguing
through the test of experience: beliefs are evaluated by their usefulness,
their consequences in practice, and their capacity to resolve genuine problems.

It produces a fallibilist theory of knowledge, a pluralist approach to truth,
and a philosophy of inquiry that treats science, ethics, and democracy as
continuous experimental practices rather than applications of fixed principles.

Pragmatism does not ground knowledge in a priori rational structures, does not
treat truth as correspondence to a mind-independent reality, and does not
separate theoretical inquiry from its practical and social context.
