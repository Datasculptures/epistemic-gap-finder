Utilitarianism holds that the morally correct action is the one that produces
the greatest total wellbeing — or the greatest happiness for the greatest
number — summed across all affected parties.

It operates in ethics and political philosophy, arguing through consequentialist
reasoning: actions are evaluated solely by their outcomes, measured in welfare,
preference satisfaction, or hedonic states depending on the variant.

It produces a decision procedure for moral and policy choices, a framework for
comparing outcomes across individuals and groups, and a foundation for welfare
economics and public health ethics.

Utilitarianism does not ground ethics in duties, rights, or character, and it
does not give special moral weight to the agent's own commitments, promises,
or relationships beyond their contribution to aggregate welfare.
