Continental philosophy is a tradition encompassing phenomenology, existentialism,
hermeneutics, critical theory, structuralism, and post-structuralism — united
less by a shared method than by a shared set of concerns: history, power,
language, interpretation, and the critique of Enlightenment reason.

It operates through historical engagement, textual interpretation, and the
critique of hidden assumptions in culture, politics, and knowledge, drawing on
thinkers from Hegel and Nietzsche through Heidegger, Foucault, and Derrida.

It produces genealogies of knowledge and power, hermeneutic methods for
interpreting texts and practices, and critical frameworks for examining how
reason, identity, and institutions are historically constituted.

Continental philosophy does not prioritise formal logical rigour as its primary
criterion of philosophical success, does not treat philosophy as an extension of
natural science, and resists reduction to the analysis of propositions divorced
from their historical and cultural context.
