Phenomenology is the philosophical study of the structure of first-person
conscious experience — it investigates how things appear to consciousness,
how experience is structured by intentionality (the directedness of mind
toward objects), and how the lived body and temporal flow shape the world
as it is given to us.

It operates in philosophy of mind, ontology, and methodology, arguing through
careful description of experience as it presents itself, bracketing theoretical
presuppositions to return to the things themselves.

It produces a methodology for describing the essential structures of experience,
accounts of perception, time, embodiment, and intersubjectivity, and a
framework that influenced psychology, cognitive science, and continental
philosophy.

Phenomenology does not ground inquiry in external scientific observation, does
not evaluate experience by its practical consequences, and does not begin from
abstract logical or mathematical principles — it insists on returning to lived
experience as the primary datum.
